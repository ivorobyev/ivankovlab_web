Database description

FitLand database contains genotype-to-phenotype maps (data and visualizations) for proteins from organisms across eukaryotes, bacteria, and viruses, which were obtained by high-throughput deep mutational scanning experiments.

 

Database organization

The basic entry of our database is a fitness landscape, which is a table of genotype-to-phenotype connections measured for a certain protein at certain conditions. Fitness landscapes are grouped in sets by the research paper where they were described.

 

Data access

Several lists of the fitness landscapes are available: all landscapes, landscapes containing both single and multiple mutants, and landscapes containing only single mutants. Independently, one can access fitness landscapes grouped by species. Search by protein name, species or publication is implemented.

 

Data visualizations

The individual page created for each fitness landscape contains information about protein, organism, experimental conditions, method of phenotype measurements, and publication. Moreover, multiple visualizations are created for each landscape, including interactive protein structure, mutations count distribution, distribution of phenotypes, position-specific distribution of phenotypes, heatmap for phenotypes of single mutations, heatmap for averaged phenotypes of mutations, and violin plots for comparing the distributions of phenotypes between genotypes with different number of mutations.

 

Data preparation

Genotype-to-phenotype maps were taken from the original publications if published. If data were published for mutations on a nucleotide level, we averaged phenotype effects over codons coding the same amino acid. In some publications, only raw read counts were available, in that case, we recalculated phenotypes ourselves but in strict accordance with the procedures described in the corresponding paper.

 

Miscellaneous

For convenient matching between original publication and our database, we kept the same position ordering and the same phenotype scaling as in the original publication. Therefore, position numbers could be different from anything else, e.g., UniProt. Moreover, when comparing fitness landscapes assayed in different publications, one should keep in mind that measures of phenotype could have different biological meanings and be scaled in different ways.
